Mathematics

The ideas behind the patterns.

Phi & Form uses visual systems that are mathematically rich but immediately explorable. This page gives the shared context: what makes Penrose tilings special, how rhombus tilings differ, and why both create such compelling structures.

At a glance

Penrose and Rhombi are related, but not the same.

Penrose tiling uses two tile types to create non-repeating order. Rhombus tiling uses three tile types to build symmetry, variation, and larger repeating fields.

Overview

Mathematics here is visual before it is verbal.

The goal is not to turn the site into a textbook. It is to help people notice what they are seeing: order without repetition, symmetry built from local choices, and structure emerging from a small number of tile rules.

That is why the homepage stays concise while this page carries the broader mathematical context.

What to look for

Three useful lenses

  • How many tile types define the system
  • Whether the pattern repeats or avoids repetition
  • How local tile rules build larger visible structure

Compare

Two apps, two kinds of geometric order.

Penrose Tiles

Non-repeating order from 2 tile types.

Penrose tiling is known for creating coherent structures that do not settle into an ordinary repeating grid. That tension between order and non-repetition is what gives the system its distinct visual character.

  • 2 tile types
  • Aperiodic structure
  • Non-repeating order

Rhombi Tiles

Symmetry and variation from 3 tile types.

Rhombus tiling works differently. By combining three tile types, it produces larger patterned fields where symmetry, repetition, and variation become easier to compare and shape directly.

  • 3 tile types
  • Rhombus tiling
  • Symmetry and variation

Connections

Different systems, shared questions.

01

How much structure can come from a few rules?

Both apps ask how simple constraints between neighboring tiles produce a much larger visible order.

02

What does repetition change in the viewer’s experience?

Penrose resists ordinary repetition, while Rhombi makes repeating symmetries easier to notice and compose with.

03

Why do geometric systems feel beautiful?

In both cases, beauty comes from the balance between constraint and surprise: enough rule to feel coherent, enough variation to stay alive.
